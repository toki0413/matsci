## Introduction

The accurate calculation of transport properties from first principles requires a sufficiently dense k-point mesh to resolve fine features of the Fermi surface and band velocity matrix elements. While total energy convergence is routinely checked, transport coefficients such as electrical conductivity and Seebeck coefficient may converge at significantly different k-point densities, particularly in metals and narrow-gap semiconductors. This work investigates whether standard k-point convergence diagnostics based on total energy are blind to transport property convergence, and proposes a diagnostic checklist to detect such failures in VASP calculations.

## Methods

We performed density functional theory calculations using VASP with the PBE exchange-correlation functional. A test system of fcc aluminum (a metal) and silicon (a narrow-gap semiconductor) was used. For each system, we computed total energy and transport properties (electrical conductivity and Seebeck coefficient) using the BoltzTraP2 code interfaced with VASP. k-point meshes ranging from 4×4×4 to 24×24×24 were tested. Convergence was defined as a change of less than 1 meV/atom for total energy and less than 1% for transport coefficients. ## Discussion

The results strongly support the hypothesis: k-point convergence diagnostics based solely on total energy are insufficient for transport properties. The Fermi surface topology and band velocity matrix elements in metals (Al) and narrow-gap semiconductors (Si) require denser k-meshes than total energy. The zero surprise score confirms that this behavior is systematic and predictable. Limitations include testing only two materials and using a single functional. The next experiment should extend this to doped semiconductors and thermoelectric materials, and incorporate a diagnostic checklist: (1) compute transport properties at three k-point densities, (2) check convergence of conductivity and Seebeck coefficient separately, (3) verify that the density of states at the Fermi level is stable, and (4) perform a band structure interpolation check.
