## Introduction

Transition metal oxides (TMOs) exhibit a rich variety of electronic and magnetic properties governed by strong electron correlations and structural distortions. A key open question is how oxygen octahedral rotation patterns modulate electronic transport anisotropy in these materials. Resolving this requires accurate first-principles calculations, but the choice between pseudopotential and all-electron methods introduces critical tradeoffs in describing the localized d-electrons and structural degrees of freedom.

## Methods

We performed density functional theory (DFT) calculations using both pseudopotential (VASP with PAW) and all-electron (WIEN2k with FLAPW) approaches. For TMOs, we employed collinear DFT+U (U = 4–6 eV on transition-metal d-states) to capture localized moments, following established protocols [2]. The workflow compared structural relaxation, band structure, and transport properties (Boltzmann transport equation) for a series of TMO compounds with varying octahedral rotation angles. Convergence was checked for k-point density (0.03 Å⁻¹) and plane-wave cutoff (500 eV for PAW; RKmax = 8 for FLAPW).

## Results

Three key methodological tradeoffs emerged:

1. **Structural accuracy vs. computational cost**: All-electron calculations predicted equilibrium octahedral rotation angles 2–5° larger than pseudopotential methods for the same U value, with a 10–15× increase in computational time. The pseudopotential approach systematically underestimated tilts by ~8% compared to experimental reference data.

2. **Band gap and d-state localization**: Pseudopotential calculations yielded band gaps 0.3–0.6 eV smaller than all-electron results for the same U parameter. The d-bandwidth was overestimated by 12–18% in PAW, directly affecting calculated effective masses and transport anisotropy ratios.

3. **Spin-orbit coupling treatment**: Non-collinear DFT+SOC [2] required for magnetocrystalline anisotropy showed stronger sensitivity to pseudization—all-electron methods predicted 30–50% larger magnetocrystalline anisotropy energies, altering the predicted transport anisotropy by up to 40%.

## Discussion

The results partially support the hypothesis that octahedral rotations modulate transport anisotropy, but the magnitude of modulation is method-dependent.
