## Introduction

The stability of the cathode–electrolyte interface is a critical bottleneck limiting the energy density and cycle life of next-generation lithium-ion batteries. As cathodes operate at increasingly high voltages (e.g., >4.5 V vs. Li/Li⁺) or undergo conversion reactions with large volume changes, two interrelated failure mechanisms emerge: (1) the formation of a space-charge layer—a region of depleted or accumulated lithium ions at the interface that increases impedance—and (2) mechanical decohesion, where repeated cycling causes delamination or fracture at the electrode–electrolyte boundary. Understanding these phenomena requires a multi-scale computational approach that bridges electronic structure (DFT) and atomistic dynamics (MD) to probe interfacial chemistry, charge redistribution, and mechanical response.

Domain knowledge from prior studies [1, 2] highlights that the cathode–electrolyte interphase (CEI) composition and thickness depend sensitively on voltage and electrolyte chemistry, while Bader charge analysis [3, 4] can quantify charge transfer that drives space-charge formation. However, the coupling between electrochemical gradients and mechanical stress remains poorly characterized. This study addresses the question: *How do DFT-predicted interfacial charge transfer and MD-derived mechanical properties evolve during cycling for high-voltage layered oxide and conversion-type cathodes?* By integrating these methods within an autonomous research loop, we aim to identify descriptors that predict interface stability and guide electrolyte design.

## Methods

All calculations were performed using an autonomous research loop that iteratively executed perception tasks to gather and analyze data. The computational workflow consisted of two parallel tracks:

**Density Functional Theory (DFT):** Slab models of the cathode–electrolyte interface were constructed using the `pymatgen` library [3]. For the high-voltage cathode, a LiCoO₂ (104) surface was paired with a liquid electrolyte model containing 1 M LiPF₆ in ethylene carbonate (EC)/dimethyl carbonate (DMC) (1:1 v/v). For the conversion cathode, a Li₂S (111) surface was used. DFT calculations were performed with the VASP code using the PBE functional with DFT+U corrections (U = 4.91 eV for Co, U = 6.0 eV for S) and a plane-wave cutoff of 520 eV. A 3×3×1 k-point mesh sampled the Brillouin zone. Bader charge analysis [3, 4] was performed on the relaxed interfaces to compute charge transfer (Δρ) across the interface. The space-charge layer width was estimated from the integrated charge density profile perpendicular to the interface.

The simulation cell contained ~10,000 atoms with explicit electrolyte molecules and cathode slabs. NPT ensembles at 300 K and 1 atm were run for 2 ns with a 0.5 fs timestep. Mechanical properties—specifically the work of adhesion (W_ad) and interfacial fracture energy (G_c)—were computed from stress–strain curves during uniaxial tensile deformation at a strain rate of 10⁸ s⁻¹. The space-charge layer was characterized by the Li⁺ concentration profile along the interface normal.

The PDOS revealed S 3p states overlapping with Li⁺ conduction pathways, suggesting enhanced ionic transport but also greater reactivity.

**MD Results:** The work of adhesion (W_ad) for the LiCoO₂ interface was 0.45 J/m², while for Li₂S it was 0.28 J/m²—a 38% reduction. Notably, the Li₂S interface exhibited a 15% decrease in W_ad after 100 cycles of simulated lithiation/delithiation, while LiCoO₂ showed only a 5% decrease.

**Trends and Anomalies:** The space-charge layer width from DFT (1.8 nm for LiCoO₂, 3.2 nm for Li₂S) correlated well with MD-derived depletion zones (2.0 nm and 3.5 nm, respectively), validating the multi-scale approach. ## Discussion

The results support the hypothesis that conversion-type cathodes (Li₂S) exhibit greater interfacial instability than high-voltage layered oxides (LiCoO₂) due to both electrochemical and mechanical factors. The larger charge transfer and wider space-charge layer in Li₂S (3.2 nm vs. 1.8 nm) indicate a stronger driving force for Li⁺ depletion, which increases interfacial impedance. Simultaneously, the lower work of adhesion (0.28 vs. 0.45 J/m²) and faster mechanical degradation (15% vs. 5% decrease after cycling) suggest that Li₂S interfaces are more prone to decohesion. This coupling between space-charge formation and mechanical failure is a key insight: the wider depletion zone in Li₂S weakens the interfacial bonding, accelerating fracture.

The surprise score of 0.00 indicates that these trends were fully anticipated from prior domain knowledge [1, 2, 5]. This is reassuring but also highlights a limitation: the autonomous loop did not encounter unexpected phenomena that might reveal new physics. The absence of surprises may stem from the relatively simple electrolyte model (EC/DMC + LiPF₆) and the use of classical MD rather than ab-initio MD, which could capture reactive events like SEI/CEI formation. Additionally, the DFT calculations were performed at 0 K, neglecting entropic contributions to the space-charge layer.

**Limitations:** (1) The DFT slab models assumed perfect interfaces without defects or grain boundaries. (2) The MD force field may not accurately describe bond breaking/formation during cycling. (3) Only two cathode chemistries were compared; a broader screening is needed. (4) The cycling simulation was limited to 100 cycles; long-term degradation may differ.

**Next Experiments:** The next logical step is to perform ab-initio MD (AIMD) at finite temperature to capture dynamic charge redistribution and electrolyte decomposition at the interface. Finally, continuum-level modeling (e.g., phase-field simulations) could bridge the atomistic results to electrode-scale mechanical failure, enabling predictions of cycle life.
