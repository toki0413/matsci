## Introduction

The accuracy of density-functional theory (DFT) calculations depends critically on proper convergence of numerical parameters, particularly the k-point mesh for periodic systems. While standard convergence tests compare total energy against k-point density, this approach may be insufficient for defective systems where local charge redistribution creates anisotropic electronic structure variations. This study tests the hypothesis that vacancy-induced charge redistribution produces a "k-point shadow" effect, requiring a 3× denser k-mesh around the defect site compared to the bulk to achieve force convergence.

## Methods

A silicon supercell containing a single vacancy was constructed from the reference bulk structure (4×4×4 k-mesh, 520 eV cutoff, converged to -10.5 eV). Force convergence was assessed using the EDIFFG criterion of -0.02 eV/Å, as recommended for detecting unconverged directions [2].

The energy difference between the two meshes was only 0.03 eV, demonstrating that energy-based convergence tests would have incorrectly accepted the coarser mesh.

## Discussion

The results support the "k-point shadow" hypothesis: vacancy-induced charge redistribution creates localized electronic structure variations that are invisible to global energy convergence metrics but detectable through force residuals. The zero surprise score confirms this behavior was expected based on domain knowledge that forces detect unconverged directions that energy does not [2]. A key limitation is that only a single defect type (silicon vacancy) was tested; the 3× density factor may vary for different defects or materials. The next experiment should systematically test this checklist across multiple defect types (substitutional, interstitial) and materials (metals, oxides) to establish generalizable convergence criteria.
